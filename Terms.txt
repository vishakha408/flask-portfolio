Revenue Growth:
How fast a company’s sales are increasing.
Growing revenue usually means the business is expanding.

Total Revenue:
Total money the company makes from selling products/services (before expenses).

Net Profit Margin:
How much profit the company keeps from its sales.
Formula: Net Income ÷ Revenue * 100
If revenue = $100
Profit = $20
Net profit margin = 20%
Higher margin = company keeps more money from each dollar earned.

Return on Equity (ROE):
How good the company is at using investors’ money to make profit.
Formula: Profit ÷ Shareholders’ money
If investors put in $100
Company makes $20 profit
ROE = 20%
Higher ROE = company uses money efficiently.

Debt to Equity (D/E):
How much debt the company has compared to its own money.
Formula: Debt ÷ Shareholders’ equity
If Debt = $50
Equity = $100
D/E = 0.5
High D/E = company relies more on borrowed money (riskier).

Balance Sheet:
A financial snapshot of a company at one moment.
It shows: Assets (what it owns), Liabilities (what it owes), Equity (what’s left for shareholders)
Formula: Assets = Liabilities + Equity

RSI (Relative Strength Index);:
Shows if a stock is overbought or oversold.
Step 1: Calculate Relative Strength (RS)
        RS = Average Gain / Average Loss
Step 2: Calculate RSI
        RSI = 100 − (100 / (1 + RS))
Standard period = 14
Scale: 0–100
Above 70 → Overbought (might drop soon)
Below 30 → Oversold (might rise soon)
Approx. 50 → Neutral

Moving Averages (MA):
Got it 👍 — you want the general formulas and concepts, not Python code.

Here’s a clear breakdown of MA, RSI, and MACD conceptually.

Moving Average (MA):
🔹 Simple Moving Average (SMA)	​
Average stock price over a period of time.
Used to: Identify trend direction, Find support/resistance, Generate buy/sell signals
Types:
SMA (Simple Moving Average) → normal average
Formula: (𝑃1 + 𝑃2 + ... Pn) / n
Where:
P = closing price of day 1,2...n
n = number of periods(days)
Example (5-day MA):
= Sum of Last 5 closing prices /5

EMA (Exponential Moving Average) → gives more weight to recent prices
Formula: EMA = (Price × k) + (Previous EMA × (1 − k))
Where:
k = 2 / (n + 1)
n = number of periods


🟢 Support = a price level where the stock usually stops falling and bounces back up.
Think of it like a floor.
When price falls to that level, buyers step in and push it back up.
If a stock keeps dropping to $50 and then going back up —
👉 $50 is acting as support.
Because buyers believe it's "cheap" at that price.

🔴 Resistance = a price level where the stock usually stops rising and falls back down.
Think of it like a ceiling.
When price rises to that level, sellers step in.
If a stock keeps rising to $80 and then dropping —
👉 $80 is acting as resistance.
Because sellers think it’s "expensive" at that price.

Break Below Support:
If price falls below support, It may keep dropping.
Old support often becomes new resistance.

Break Above Resistance:
If price breaks above resistance, It may keep rising.
Old resistance often becomes new support.

This is called a breakout.

MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence):
A trend-following indicator made from moving averages.
🔵 MACD Line:
Difference between two moving averages (usually 12-day & 26-day).
Shows trend direction.
🟠 Signal Line
A smoothed average of the MACD line.
Used to spot buy/sell signals.

MACD Line = EMA(12) − EMA(26)
Signal Line = EMA(9) of MACD ------- i.e 9-period EMA of MACD
Histogram = MACD − Signal Line

MACD crosses above Signal → Possible buy BULLISH
MACD crosses below Signal → Possible sell BEARISH
Large histogram → Strong momentum

EPS (Earnings Per Share):
Formula: Profit ÷ number of shares.
Shows how much profit each share earns.

Volume:
Number of shares traded.
High volume = strong movement.

P/E Ratio (Price to Earnings):
Formula: Stock price ÷ EPS
Shows if a stock is expensive or cheap compared to earnings.

Market Cap:
Total value of the company.
Formula: Share price × total shares.
